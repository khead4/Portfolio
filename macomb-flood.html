Three Site Comparisons

What does the existing landscape look like? Three flood-adjacent resources evaluated for how well they serve a resident in crisis — examining structure, language, GIS access, and coverage guidance.

FloodSmart.gov
floodsmart.gov — FEMA's NFIP information site
What it does well

Comprehensive NFIP policy information, claim initiation links, and adjuster contact guidance. Authoritative source for what flood insurance covers.

What it lacks

No guidance for residents without flood insurance

No sewer backup or homeowners coverage routing

Policy language is accurate but not crisis-readable

No documentation guidance or packet output

No local Macomb resources or GIS integration

Useful as a reference once the user already knows they have NFIP coverage. Not useful as a first contact during a crisis.
DisasterAssistance.gov
disasterassistance.gov — FEMA individual assistance
What it does well

Cross-agency assistance navigator, connects residents to FEMA IA, SBA loans, unemployment, and other aid programs in one place.

What it lacks

Requires a presidentially declared disaster to unlock most benefits

No insurance coverage guidance — assumes aid is the only path

No local Michigan or Macomb-specific resources

No photo documentation tools or claim packet output

Dense registration form front-loaded before any help

Most useful after a major declared disaster. Leaves residents stranded during local flood events that do not meet the declaration threshold.
Michigan EGLE Flood Resources
michigan.gov/egle — state environmental and floodplain guidance
What it does well

Authoritative floodplain mapping, permit guidance, FEMA map links, and environmental data for Michigan. Strong for regulatory and professional use.

What it lacks

Written for engineers, planners, and permit officers — not residents

No insurance coverage guidance at all

No step-by-step recovery flow

GIS data requires technical knowledge to interpret

No community resource directory or assistance routing

Excellent as a technical reference but inaccessible to a resident under stress. The GIS portal requires prior knowledge of what a "map layer" means and how to use ArcGIS.
Gap summary

What all three sites miss together

No single existing resource covers: cause-based insurance routing, time-critical documentation guidance before cleanup, integrated GIS from multiple sources, local Macomb-specific assistance contacts, progressive disclosure for a crisis context, and a downloadable packet. The Macomb Flood Response Platform was built specifically to fill that gap — not to replace these sources but to serve as the first-contact tool that routes to them correctly.

Tone & Purpose

A flood response platform is not a marketing site. It is not a legal document. It is not a government portal. Understanding what it must be — and what it must not feel like — shaped every writing decision.

Tone
Calm, direct, present-tense

The voice is second-person and present-tense throughout — "you," "your property," "your next step." Not third-person case descriptions. Not conditional passive constructions. The platform acknowledges emotional reality first, then moves to action. It never adds urgency that the situation does not already contain.

"You do not have to solve everything at once. Start with safety, then let the site help organize the rest."
"What to photograph when it is safe — photos turn memory into proof."
Purpose
Organize, not decide

The platform explicitly states: it is a decision aid, not a legal or policy determination. It routes toward the right question to ask an insurer or caseworker — not toward the answer. This distinction is not a disclaimer. It is the purpose of every coverage section: give the resident enough structure to act, not enough certainty to skip professional review.

"Coverage guidance is a decision aid only. Final coverage depends on the policy, endorsements, exclusions, damage cause, adjuster review, and official eligibility rules."
Voice register
Plain, not simplified

Plain language preserves nuance while removing unnecessary difficulty. The platform explains NFIP without using "NFIP." It explains sewer backup endorsements without assuming the reader knows what an endorsement is. Terms are introduced in plain language first, then named formally in parentheses if needed for cross-referencing with policy documents.

What was deliberately avoided
Urgency, optimism, and paternalism

The platform does not say "act now," "you've got this," or "don't worry." Urgency is already present in the situation. False optimism reduces trust. Paternalistic reassurance ignores the real difficulty. The platform treats the reader as competent enough to handle accurate information delivered clearly — which is a higher form of respect than softening the content.

"Minimum code compliance is not the same as flood-ready. Compare the home against freeboard, raised utilities, drainage, backflow, flood-resistant materials, and safe access."

Crazy 8

Eight rapid interface concepts sketched in 8 minutes. The constraint: each must solve the same problem — getting a resident in crisis to the right next step — from a completely different structural angle.

Concept 01
Binary branch entry

Single first question routes the entire interface. Emergency or recovery — nothing else on screen.

Concept 02
Issue router accordion

Single-select list opens one section at a time. No dashboard — no simultaneous overwhelm.

Concept 03
Photo-first entry

Upload before anything else. Platform builds the inventory from visual evidence, then surfaces guidance from what it finds.

Concept 04
Wizard with progress

Linear 5-step flow. Visible progress reduces anxiety. Each question unlocks the next only when answered.

Concept 05
Map-first entry

Drop a pin on your address. Platform loads FEMA zone, elevation, and drain proximity before any form appears.

Concept 06
Recovery timeline

Time-based structure: hour 0, hour 1, day 1, day 3. Each stage reveals only the actions relevant to that window.

Concept 07
Source-first routing

Ask damage source before everything. Answer determines the insurance path — all other content follows from that single variable.

Concept 08
Packet-first structure

Show the empty output packet first. Every subsequent tool feeds it. The resident always sees what they are building toward.

What was chosen and why

Concepts 01 + 02 combined — binary branch into issue router

The binary branch (Concept 01) handles the most critical UX problem: a resident in genuine danger should not see insurance forms. The issue router (Concept 02) handles the second most critical problem: a resident not in danger still does not need to see eight things simultaneously. Combining them produces the platform's actual entry flow — one safety question, then one-at-a-time issue routing. The packet is present throughout as a persistent counter but does not lead the experience.

Design principle

The visual system serves legibility under pressure, not brand identity.

Every decision in this style guide traces back to a single constraint: the platform must be usable by someone standing in a flooded basement, reading on a phone, with one hand, under high stress. That means large enough touch targets, high enough contrast, short enough labels, and a visual hierarchy that makes the next action immediately obvious — regardless of whether the user has previously visited the site.

Data Charts

Key data that shaped platform design decisions — flood claim denial rates, FEMA vs actual risk gaps, documentation timing windows, and Macomb County flood event history. Each chart reveals a gap between existing systems and resident needs.

Chart 01 — Source: NFIP Claim Data; FEMA Claims Handbook; Insurance Information Institute

Flood Insurance Claim Issues — Why Residents Struggle

The most common reasons flood claims are reduced, disputed, or require additional documentation. Each of these directly informed a platform feature — the photo checklist addresses documentation gaps, the coverage engine addresses policy confusion, and the intake form captures cause-of-damage before cleanup begins.

Data synthesised from FEMA NFIP claim adjuster reports, Insurance Information Institute flood claims analysis, and Michigan DIFS consumer guidance. Percentages represent proportional frequency of issue type among disputed or reduced claims — not absolute denial rate. Each issue type directly maps to a platform feature designed to address it before the claim is filed.

Chart 02 — Source: FEMA Adjuster Guidelines; Red Cross Disaster Response Timing Data

The Documentation Window — Hours That Determine Claim Outcomes

Evidence quality drops sharply as cleanup begins. The platform's photo checklist and inventory tools are designed to be used during the first six hours after flooding — when damage is visible but before it is removed. This chart shows why timing is the most important variable in flood claim support.

The "documentation window" is not a metaphor. The specific evidence types that appear on the platform's photo checklist — water lines, appliance positions, serial numbers, electrical panel state, sump pump status — each exist for a specific number of hours after flooding. The platform positions the checklist as the first action after safety is confirmed, not after insurance contact or cleanup decisions are made.

Chart 03 — Source: NFIP Policy Manual; Michigan DIFS; Insurance Information Institute

Coverage Route by Damage Cause

Which policy applies depends entirely on where the water came from — not where it ended up or how much damage it caused. This is the core complexity the coverage engine was built to navigate.

This matrix is the structural core of the platform's coverage engine. The damage cause question in intake is positioned first because every subsequent coverage routing depends on it. Source: NFIP Policy Manual; Michigan DIFS consumer guidance; Insurance Information Institute flood insurance explainer.
