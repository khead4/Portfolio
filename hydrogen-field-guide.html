Three Site Comparisons

Three existing hydrogen information resources evaluated against the needs of the Hydrogen Field Guide's audience — examining source transparency, pathway comparison depth, tone, and usability for non-specialist readers.

energy.gov — Hydrogen
energy.gov/cmei/fuels/hydrogen — DOE hydrogen program
What it does well

Primary source for technical targets, PEM electrolysis specifications, and production pathway cost targets. Authoritative, well-documented, and regularly updated.

What it lacks

Assumes specialist technical background throughout

No structured comparison of pathways — each is on a separate page

No place-based cost examples — only national targets

Safety section is technical and regulatory, not design-oriented

No narrative framing — data without structure for decision-making

Essential as the primary source database, but not structured for an investor, policy writer, or procurement officer reading in a decision context.
IEA Hydrogen Review
iea.org — Global Hydrogen Review (annual)
What it does well

Comprehensive global market framing, demand/supply data, and policy landscape. Credible, rigorous, and appropriately cautious about low-emissions supply share.

What it lacks

Very long — 200+ page report format, hard to navigate for quick reference

No pathway-by-pathway visual comparison

No place-based cost examples with local context

Fuel cell electrochemistry not covered — only market framing

Executive summary written for policy audience, not accelerator context

Best for macro market framing and cautious demand claims. Too long and too policy-oriented to serve as a first-read reference for accelerator evaluation work.
Hydrogen Council
hydrogencouncil.com — industry coalition
What it does well

Accessible language, strong visual design, covers mobility and infrastructure use cases. Good for general awareness and getting an overview of the ecosystem quickly.

What it lacks

Industry coalition — fundamentally promotional, not neutral

No primary source anchoring — claims are not traceable

Blue and grey hydrogen framed optimistically throughout

No safety framing — only positive use case coverage

Pathway efficiency claims are not sourced to specific data sets

Visually strong and accessible — but not a credible reference for due diligence. Its promotional framing is precisely what the Field Guide was designed to counterbalance.
Gap summary

What all three miss together

No existing resource offers: a structured four-pathway visual comparison, place-based cost examples with explanatory context, sourced efficiency figures with explicit uncertainty ranges, a fuel cell electrochemistry primer accessible to non-chemists, and a safety framing that is neither alarmist nor dismissive. The Field Guide was built to serve that specific reading context — evidence-led, traceable to primary sources, accessible without specialist knowledge.

Tone & Purpose

A research guide for an accelerator audience fails if it reads like promotional content or like an academic paper. Understanding the specific register required — and what tone decisions flow from that — shaped every writing and design choice in the guide.

Tone
Precise, conditional, not promotional

The guide is written as though it is presenting evidence to a skeptical but open-minded reader. Every claim includes either a source anchor or an explicit uncertainty range. Conditional language is used where the evidence is conditional — "blue hydrogen is a transition argument, not a final answer" rather than "blue hydrogen is a promising bridge technology."

"Grey and brown hydrogen can look attractive because the fuel supply chains already exist... The problem is that the cheap price usually leaves out climate damage, upstream methane leakage, coal mining impacts, carbon dioxide emissions, and the cost of eventually replacing the system."
Purpose
Equip evaluation, not advocate a conclusion

The guide does not conclude that hydrogen is the future or that it is overhyped. It gives the reader the structural framework — production pathways, cost variables, electrochemistry, safety design — to reach their own evaluation-relevant conclusion. For an accelerator, that is the more useful document. An advocacy brief has a predetermined answer; a field guide gives you the tools to find your own.

"The molecule is identical in every pathway, so the real comparison is upstream. Green wins on climate value, grey wins on near-term access, blue depends on measured carbon capture, and brown carries the weakest long-term case."
Voice register
Technical accuracy without technical gatekeeping

The half-reactions are stated correctly (H₂ → 2H⁺ + 2e⁻ at the anode). The efficiency figures are drawn from DOE datasets. But the surrounding prose explains why each fact matters in plain language — "the gap between the reversible minimum voltage (1.23V) and the thermoneutral voltage (1.48V) is why stored hydrogen energy is always less than the electricity put in."

What was deliberately avoided
Clean energy optimism and techno-skeptic dismissal

The guide frames hydrogen's promise and its problem as structurally linked — they come from the same physical property (low molecular weight). This prevents both the "hydrogen will solve everything" framing and the "hydrogen is too hard and too expensive" dismissal. Both are intellectually dishonest for different reasons, and both would fail an accelerator audience that needs to make an actual decision.

"Hydrogen's value comes from storing energy in a light molecule; its challenge comes from needing pressure, cold, compatible materials, and a clean production route before that molecule becomes useful."

Crazy 8

Eight rapid structure concepts for communicating complex technical evidence to a non-specialist audience. The constraint: each must make the pathway comparison legible in under 30 seconds, from a completely different structural angle.

Concept 01
Four-column colour grid

Side-by-side columns for instant pathway comparison. Color identifies, efficiency anchors the number, label names the strength or weakness.

Concept 02
Narrative staircase

Linear flow from element → pathways → cost → safety. Each page builds understanding required for the next. Reader cannot skip ahead without context loss.

Concept 03
Scenario decision matrix

Scenario → pathway → rationale. Turns the guide into a decision tool. Useful for accelerator evaluation context where the reader has a specific use case.

Concept 04
Supply chain diagram

Three rows, each showing input → conversion → output for one pathway. The CO₂ emissions point is visible at the end of each row.

Concept 05
Location price map

Pin locations on a map with cost and pathway type. Makes the geographic variation in hydrogen economics immediately intuitive.

Concept 06
Claim + source cards

Every claim paired with its primary source as the primary UI. Turns the source section into the organizing structure of the whole guide.

Concept 07
Chemistry-first structure

Lead with the fuel cell reaction. Once the reader understands what H₂ does at the cathode, all pathway comparisons are grounded in physical reality.

Concept 08
Tab-switched detail panel

One pathway visible at a time in depth. Tabs switch between pathways. Full detail per pathway without overwhelming comparison overhead.

What was chosen and why

Concepts 01 + 02 + 07 combined — narrative staircase with colour grid and fuel cell primer

The guide uses a three-page structure (Concept 02) because the accelerator audience includes both detailed readers and scanners — each page works standalone. The colour grid (Concept 01) is embedded on the Element page as the comparison anchor. The fuel cell primer (Concept 07) leads the Element page because understanding what H₂ does physically grounds every pathway claim that follows. The claim + source structure (Concept 06) organises the source section throughout all three pages.

Design principle

The visual system does not have a point of view on the answer.

Every colour decision, layout choice, and typographic hierarchy in this guide was made to present evidence neutrally — not to advocate for one hydrogen pathway over another. The pathway colours are visually equivalent in weight. The efficiency comparison bars are measured from the same baseline. The source cards are indistinguishable in visual treatment regardless of which pathway they support. The guide's point of view is structural, not evaluative: here is the evidence, organized so you can reach your own conclusion.

Data Charts

Key data visualised from the field guide's primary sources — production costs, pathway efficiency, fuel cell energy flow, and global hydrogen adoption. Each chart directly supports a claim made in the guide.

Chart 01 — Source: IEA Global Hydrogen Review 2025, DOE NETL

Global H₂ Production Cost by Pathway & Location

Plant-gate production costs in USD/kg. These are not pump prices — compression, delivery, and dispensing add further cost. The gap between green and grey narrows in regions with cheap renewable electricity and widens where grid power is expensive.

Sources: IEA Global Hydrogen Review 2025; DOE H₂ Program cost analysis; NETL gasification benchmarks. Ranges reflect variability in electricity price, electrolyser capital cost, utilisation rate, and local gas price. Plant-gate only — excludes compression, transport, dispensing.

Chart 02 — Source: DOE PEM Electrolysis Technical Targets; NREL FCEV Analysis

Fuel Cell Energy Flow — Input to Wheel

The full energy chain from renewable electricity to vehicle motion, showing where losses occur at each conversion step. Green hydrogen has a lower round-trip efficiency than direct battery EVs (~77–80%) — this chart makes that tradeoff visible.

Sources: DOE PEM electrolysis targets (~63% efficiency); NREL hydrogen compression energy ~3%; DOE fuel cell efficiency at rated power ~55–65% (used 60%); drivetrain losses ~5%. Battery EV round-trip efficiency ~77–80% from grid to wheel for comparison. FCHEV and BEV comparisons must account for different grid energy mix assumptions.
