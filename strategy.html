Strategy

Goals, positioning,
and evidence-based decisions

Strategy is the work that happens before design — defining what a product is for, who it is for, and what the structure needs to communicate before anything is built. The work here begins with a problem and ends with a set of decisions that constrain everything that follows.

Strategic problem

Botanic Elixir is a novel concept with no direct category. Users cannot be expected to find it through conventional search behavior.

The site cannot function as a promotional page — it must function as an explanation. The structural and content decisions follow from this. Everything is oriented toward helping a visitor understand the concept before they are asked to do anything.

Central strategic theme

"Intentional Experience Through Adaptive Design" — every page title, keyword cluster, navigation label, and filtering mechanism was derived from this positioning.

This is not a tagline. It is a constraint. It ruled out product-first navigation, standard café copy conventions, and any framing that reduced the concept to its drinks menu.

Structural decisions from strategy
  • Non-linear navigation — visitors arrive at different decision stages
  • How It Works page — required because the concept is novel
  • Filtering on Experiences, Menu, and Community — content volume demands it
  • Mood-based entry — routes visitors by intent, not by product category

Outcome: strategy produced a set of structural requirements that made the IA and copy decisions non-arbitrary. Each one traces back to a defined goal.

02Audience Research · Persona Development
Who the site was built for
Three audience types defined before any layout decision was made
Audience Research Personas Decision Filters
Strategic problem

A site built for everyone is built for no one. Audience definition is not a UX artifact — it is a strategic constraint that determines what content exists and how it is structured.

How audience shapes structure

Each audience type arrived with a different primary question. The structure of the site is organized around answering those questions in the right sequence:

  • Experience Seekers need to feel oriented before they choose
  • Wellness Community members need the concept validated before they invest
  • Professionals need to understand the system before they trust it

Audience research is not descriptive. It is architectural. The personas defined which pages needed to exist, what order content appeared in, and which questions the navigation had to answer.

Connection to design

The mood selector serves Experience Seekers. How It Works serves Professionals. The Research and Science pages serve both the Wellness Community and Professionals. Each structural decision has an audience behind it.

Outcome: three audience types functioning as design constraints — not descriptions, but filters for every layout and copy decision.

Primary Audience
The Experience Seeker
Urban professionals aged 25–40 seeking intentional social and creative experiences. Value curated environments. Already avoiding alcohol or reducing consumption. Looking for something that gives a night out a purpose beyond the drink.
Primary need: understand what this is and whether it's for them — before arrival, not during.
Secondary Audience
The Wellness Community
Mindfulness practitioners, yoga and meditation communities, people building intentional daily routines. Receptive to functional ingredients and adaptive environments. Prioritize substance — they will read the research pages.
Primary need: validation that the concept is serious and evidence-based, not trend-adjacent.
Tertiary Audience
The Creative Professional
Writers, designers, developers, and creatives who need a productive environment outside the home or office. Looking for focus infrastructure — the physical space, the serum to support it, and the social permission to work in public.
Primary need: understand the system well enough to trust it before committing a working session to it.
Shared need across all three
Understanding before choice
All three audiences arrive without a clear decision already made. The site must explain the concept, establish trust, and make the choice feel obvious — in that order. None can be sent directly to a booking page.
This is the strategic reason for the How It Works page, the depth content, and the non-linear navigation structure.
03Information Architecture · Storyboard · Botanic Elixir
A non-linear structure that reflects the space
Site architecture derived directly from audience needs and strategic goals

Strategic problem

A novel concept with multiple audience types cannot use a standard hierarchical navigation model. Different users arrive at different decision stages.

A linear funnel assumes a single entry point and a shared decision sequence. Botanic Elixir's three audiences each have a different primary question — and none of them follows the same path.

Structural decisions

Non-linear navigation — every page accessible from every other. Users are not forced through a single path.

Mood-based entry — the home page routes by intent state, not product category. The first choice a user makes is about themselves, not the product.

Filtering on content-heavy pages — Experiences, Menu, and Community all have enough content that unfiltered lists would break comprehension.

How It Works as a required page — the concept is novel. The page exists because the strategy required it, not because it was a convention to follow.

What the storyboard established

The storyboard is not a wireframe. It is a proof that the structure can support every user's journey — that no audience type hits a dead end, and that every primary question gets answered before a decision is required.

Outcome: an IA that is arguable — every page exists for a stated reason, every navigation path serves a defined audience need.

04UX/UI Design Specialist · ISR Division Systems LLC · 2023–Present
Systems strategy in production
End-to-end UX design across IoT B2B workflows — from customer-facing interfaces to internal operations
B2B IoT Systems Design End-to-End Workflow Architecture Multi-System
Context

ISR Division Systems LLC is a B2B IoT company. The UX work spans the full operational surface — not a single product, but an interconnected set of systems that each need to be coherent individually and consistent with each other.

Strategic challenge

B2B systems serve users who cannot disengage from the product — logistics coordinators, repair technicians, and customer service teams use these interfaces under time pressure, often with incomplete information. The cost of a bad UX decision is measured in operational time, not bounce rate.

The strategic work here is not positioning or audience research. It is systems coherence — ensuring that decisions made in one interface do not create problems in another, and that workflow logic is embedded in the structure rather than delegated to documentation.

What this work demonstrates
  • Strategy applied to constrained, real-world operational contexts
  • Multi-system IA where consistency is a design requirement
  • Evidence-based decisions under production conditions
  • Stakeholder alignment across front-end, logistics, and service teams

Outcome: production UX across five interconnected systems, maintained and iterated over two years.

The structure of logistics workflows was designed around task completion under time pressure — status visibility, action clarity, and exception handling that doesn't require supervisor escalation for standard cases.

Service DesignWorkflowResolution
Customer service interfaces need to surface the right information at the right moment — account history, device status, prior interactions — without requiring agents to navigate between systems. Design work here focused on information consolidation and the reduction of context-switching during live support interactions.
System 04

Field operations
DispatchField UXMobile
Repair dispatch operates across mobile and desktop. Technicians need task assignments, location data, and job status available at a glance — without reading. The interface was designed for operational environments where screen time is limited and error recovery is expensive. Structural decisions here connect back to how logistics and customer service systems communicate job state.
System 05
Sign-Off Workflows
Compliance & verification
ComplianceVerificationAudit Trail
Sign-off workflows require that users confirm completion of steps that carry operational and legal weight. The design challenge is making confirmation feel like a genuine checkpoint — not a form to dismiss. Structurally, this means reducing ambiguity about what is being confirmed, and surfacing the consequences of sign-off in the interface itself.
