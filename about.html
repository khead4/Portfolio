02Methodology & Philosophy
How the work gets done
A set of commitments that constrain every project from the first question to the final build
01
Starting point
Research before structure
No layout decision is made before the audience is defined â specifically: who arrives, with what internal state, and what question they need answered before they can act. Personas, interviews, and competitive analysis are not deliverables. They are the material that design is cut from.

The corollary: if a design decision cannot be traced to a research finding or a defined user need, it is decoration. Decoration is removed.

Result: designs that are structurally grounded from the first decision â reducing revision cycles and stakeholder misalignment.
02
Information architecture
Structure is a decision
Every page exists because something requires it. Every navigation label exists because a user has a question that label answers. Every filter exists because content volume demands it. These are structural decisions â and structural decisions are always arguable.

The question I apply to every element: what would break if this wasn't here? If the answer is nothing, it's removed.

Result: leaner information architectures where every element earns its place â improving user orientation and reducing bounce.
03
Language
Copy is architecture, not content
The words chosen for a navigation label determine what kind of page is required. "How It Works" demands an explanatory sequence. "Experiences" demands a filterable list. Copy written before design produces structure. Copy added after produces decoration.

This is the discipline from a background in English: language is structural. It shapes what can be built, not just what appears on the surface.

Result: content that does structural work â reducing the need for tooltips, modals, and onboarding overlays.
04
Cognitive load
Remove the interpretive burden
When content is dense but unstructured, the burden of interpretation shifts to the user. Most users don't perform this work. The result is abandonment, misinterpretation, or incomplete understanding â not because the content is bad, but because the structure can't support it.

Design is the act of taking that burden away. Every card system, every staged interaction, every color code that replaces a label â these are tools for reducing the cognitive work required to understand something.

Result: interfaces where users move forward with confidence rather than hesitating at decision points.
05
Accessibility
Structural, not supplementary
Accessibility decisions made at the end of a project are workarounds. Accessibility decisions made at the structural level â contrast ratios in the color system, semantic HTML in the component spec, keyboard navigation in the IA â are design. A usable interface and an accessible one are the same thing.

Result: products that comply with WCAG from the first component, not as a retrofit â saving time and expanding reach.
06
Documentation
Systems that teach their own logic
A design system that presents outcomes without reasoning can be replicated, but not extended. When a designer can only copy a decision â not understand why it was made â they can't adapt it when conditions change.

Every annotated style guide, every documented decision in this portfolio is built on this principle: the artifact should transfer the reasoning, not just the result.

Result: teams that can extend the system without the original designer in the room.

05Ethos for Creating
What guides the work before the brief
The commitments that are non-negotiable regardless of project, client, or timeline
◇
Argue for your decisions
Every design decision should be defensible â not just defended. Defensible means you can state the reasoning clearly. If you can't, you're guessing, and guessing is not design.
◆
Defer to the user, not the aesthetic
Visual beauty is a consequence of clear thinking, not a goal. When a decision looks better but works worse, it isn't a gain. Design's purpose is to reduce friction between a person and what they're trying to do.
△
Start with the question, not the answer
The most dangerous moment in a project is when you think you already know what the user needs. Assumptions close off what could be truer. The research phase exists to surface what you were wrong about â before those assumptions get built into the product.
▽
Complexity is a failure mode
When a system is hard to use, the temptation is to add explanation â tutorials, tooltips, onboarding flows. These are symptoms of structural failure. The correct response is redesign. Explanation defers the problem; redesign solves it.
⬢
Accessibility is not an add-on
An interface that excludes people with disabilities is incomplete. Accessibility isn't a checklist at the end â it's a constraint from the beginning. Semantic structure, contrast ratios, keyboard navigation: these are design decisions, not compliance tasks.
◯
Transfer the reasoning, not just the result
Documentation that shows what was decided is less useful than documentation that shows why. A team that understands the reasoning can extend the system. A team that only has the outputs can only imitate them â the difference between a prescriptive system and a generative one.
