Discipline · Product Design

Designing systems that feel
clear and intentional

The design work here is about hierarchy, pathways, atmosphere, and interaction. It turns research and strategy into forms people can actually move through, understand, and act on.

See Case Study Research
Design Position
A restrained interface can still feel rich when the structure is carrying meaning.

The aim is not maximal chrome. It is purposeful guidance: enough hierarchy, enough contrast, and enough atmosphere to make the product legible without flattening it.

Focus

What this design work
optimizes for

The central question is how to make an unfamiliar or layered product understandable on first contact while preserving tone, identity, and depth.

Hierarchy

Guide attention

Not every element should compete equally. Structure decides what is seen first, what supports it, and what can recede.

Navigation

Support paths

Some visitors browse openly. Others arrive with a direct task. Design has to support both without creating friction.

Atmosphere

Signal experience

Color, image treatment, spacing, and motion communicate what a product feels like before a paragraph is read.

Clarity

Lower hesitation

Good product design reduces cognitive work at decision points and makes the next step visible without becoming mechanical.

Principles

How design decisions
are made

Visual polish matters, but only when the underlying logic is already working. Design is judged by what it does for understanding, movement, and trust.

Interface should explain

Navigation labels, grouping, and sequence all contribute to explanation. Design is not separate from content; it determines whether content becomes usable.

That is especially important in products that need interpretation before selection.

Atmosphere should support function

The strongest aesthetic systems intensify understanding rather than hiding it. Visual restraint only works when readability and orientation are still protected.

Accessibility is part of the standard, not an afterthought.

Process

From structure to
visual system

The work moves from audience and task clarity into wireframes, interaction patterns, visual language, and refinement through implementation.

01

Frame

Define the decision points users must move through and what information each point needs.

02

Organize

Shape content into sections, paths, and layouts that support both scanning and deeper exploration.

03

Signal

Use color, spacing, and imagery selectively so mood and meaning become visible quickly.

04

Refine

Test legibility, continuity, responsiveness, and accessibility so the final layer strengthens the system.

Connection

Where this work
connects outward

Design here is part of a larger chain. Research identifies the problem, writing gives the system language, and strategy defines what each page or flow is responsible for.

Botanic Elixir

The strongest design example uses a receding interface, mood-coded color system, and experience-based pathways to make a novel concept feel understandable before arrival.

The visual language is doing structural work, not just decorative work.

Cross-disciplinary impact

A filter is better when it reflects observed user intent. A label is better when the writing is specific. A layout is better when strategy defines the job of the page.

That is why these disciplines are linked rather than isolated.

Design in this portfolio is less about ornament and more about making complexity feel understandable, directional, and worth engaging with.
