01 — Research

Understanding who arrives
and why

Research started with two questions: who arrives, and what do they need before they can act. The audience is London residents — young creatives, students, and professionals drawn to intentional experiences, non-alcoholic drinks, and spaces that feel considered.

Most arrive looking for one of three things: upcoming experiences, an explanation of how the mood-based serums work, or practical details — location, hours, reservations. The site needed to serve all three without making any one feel secondary.

This creates a structural challenge: the system must support multiple entry points without overwhelming the user.

The structure of the site determines how the concept is understood.

Three coffee sites. What they do well — and where they stop.

Three coffee shop websites across three cities were evaluated using Nielsen Norman Group's ten usability heuristics — to understand the conventions of the category, where those conventions fail users, and where Botanic Elixir has an opportunity to do something meaningfully different.

heartroasters.com
Heart Roasters
Portland, Oregon
✓ Strengths

Strong minimalist design. Clean rotating hero avoids overwhelming the user. Product imagery (subscription box showing two coffee bags) communicates the concept immediately without text. Navigation — Shop, Locations, Learn, Wholesale — uses conventional e-commerce categories that are instantly legible to first-time visitors.

✕ Weaknesses

Carousel provides no system status — users cannot tell how many slides exist or control the sequence. "SUBSCRIBEANDSAVE" rendered without spaces forces users to decode rather than read. Navigation dots are small and visually subtle, reducing discoverability of carousel scope.

What this informed

Botanic Elixir's navigation labels were written so their meaning is unambiguous before clicking. No concatenated CTAs. The mood selector replaces a carousel with an active choice — visitors declare what they need rather than passively watching content rotate.

Heuristics: #1 Visibility of System Status · #2 Match with Real World · #6 Recognition over Recall
wormholecoffee.com
The Wormhole Coffee
Chicago, Illinois
✓ Strengths

Strong personality and atmosphere. The blog ("Bloggage") section uses café interior photography effectively, communicating the physical environment to visitors who haven't been. Job listings use language that reflects genuine character and community values — the copy reflects who the business is, not just what it sells.

✕ Weaknesses

Homepage lacks visible content on load — no feedback that the site is functioning, violating Heuristic #1 immediately. Layout breaks at non-standard zoom levels, exposing empty space that makes the site appear unfinished. Coffee product information redirects to Halfwit Coffee Roasters without explanation, breaking continuity.

What this informed

Botanic Elixir's homepage loads with immediate visual substance — the mood selector and hero communicate the concept from the first frame. All links stay within the site. The visual style communicates personality, but not at the expense of structure or load feedback.

Heuristics: #1 Visibility of Status · #4 Consistency & Standards · #7 Flexibility of Use
seycoffee.com
SEY Coffee
Brooklyn, New York
✓ Strengths

Visually cohesive and refined. Uniform product grid layouts allow rapid comparison without requiring recall from previous pages. Subscription page gives users full control — quantity, pricing, add-ons — without leaving the page. The photographic header (coffee plantation) connects the product to its real-world origin, supporting Heuristic #2 without being heavy-handed.

✕ Weaknesses

Font size is consistently small throughout — the minimalist aesthetic trades legibility for visual refinement. Users with visual impairments or on smaller screens must zoom, disrupting the layout. Aesthetics prioritised over accessibility in a way that could exclude users. A deliberate aesthetic choice that creates a structural accessibility failure.

What this informed

SEY demonstrates that a receding interface (minimal chrome, dark background, small type) only works when legibility is protected. Botanic Elixir uses the same visual restraint but treats type size and contrast as non-negotiable — the atmosphere is achieved through colour and imagery, not by compromising readability.

Heuristics: #5 Error Prevention · #7 Flexibility & Efficiency · Accessibility (WCAG)

02 — Strategy

From goals to structure

The central strategic theme was "Intentional Experience Through Adaptive Design." Every page title, keyword, and navigation decision was shaped by this. The site is not a promotional site — it is a planning and discovery tool. Its structure, language, and interactions are organised around helping visitors understand the concept, select experiences, and actively shape their upcoming visit.

The Experiences, Menu, and Community pages use filtering because they contain enough content that unfiltered lists would overwhelm. Every page is accessible from every other page through persistent header navigation — a non-linear structure that reflects the fact that visitors arrive at different points in their decision-making process.

The How It Works page is a direct response to the central challenge: this is a novel concept. Before a visitor decides whether to come, they need to understand what the space is, how the serums function, and what choosing an experience actually involves. That page exists to answer questions before they become obstacles.

03 — Writing

Language shapes what
the space communicates

Every word on the Botanic Elixir site was written to communicate what the space is before a visitor makes a choice. Language needed to be clear enough to inform and atmospheric enough to feel like the environment itself — two requirements in constant tension, and every copy decision lived in that tension.

Navigation labels were written so a visitor knows exactly what they will find before clicking. Experience descriptions answer "is this for me?" not "what is this?" Menu copy explains what a serum does to the mind and body, not just what it contains.

Copy was not content added to a completed design — it was the material the design was built from. "How It Works" as a label requires an explanatory page. "Experiences" requires a filterable list. "Community" requires recurring, living content. The words chose the structure.

Navigation Labels — Principle

"How It Works" answers the question a first-time visitor actually has. "About Our Space" does not. Every label was written to communicate function, not category. The navigation is part of the explanation of the concept — not separate from it.

Experience Copy — Writing Sessions

Experience Copy — Date Night

04 — IA & Flows

A non-linear structure
that reflects the space

Every page connects to every other through a persistent header. Visitors can move to information, exploration, or planning at any point — the structure does not assume a single path through the concept.

The Experiences, Menu, and Community pages all use filtering because they contain enough content that unfiltered lists would overwhelm. Visitors narrow to what fits their purpose without reading everything. The "How It Works" page is structured as a four-step explanatory journey because the concept requires patient explanation before a visitor can make an informed choice.

The mood selector on the home page is the site's primary act of adaptation. Choosing a mood routes the visitor to a filtered view of matching experiences — intention-first navigation, not category browsing.

UI Language

Each experience category is colour-coded with a vivid accent bar across the top of every card. These bars are the primary wayfinding system across the full site — visible in Experiences, Community events, Menu, and the calendar.

The interface recedes: dark backgrounds, minimal chrome, low-contrast borders. Mood imagery and content are the foreground. Navigation uses all-caps letter-spaced text. No decorative elements compete with the atmospheric photography.

Key Design Decisions

Navigation
Text-based header navigation, persistent across all pagesA limited page count means a search function is unnecessary and would add cognitive load. Text navigation communicates what each section contains without ambiguity. The header is always present so no visitor is ever stranded.
Mood Selector
Home page routes visitors by declared mood, not by browsingThe "Choose Your Mood" interface on the home page is the site's primary act of adaptation. Visitors declare what they came for — Focus & Create, Play & Engage, Connect & Share, Reflect & Restore — and the site responds by filtering Experiences to match. This is intention-first navigation.
Colour Coding
Vivid experience accent bars as the primary wayfinding systemBlue, violet, green, emerald, orange, pink, teal — each experience and mood category has a consistent colour across every context it appears: card tops, filter tabs, calendar dots, event badges. Visitors learn the system once and navigate by colour recognition thereafter.
Imagery
Atmospheric photography as the dominant visual elementInk in water, macro botanical details, light diffused through glass — the photography communicates mood and sensory experience before any text is processed. The UI recedes to make space for this. The site's visual intelligence lives in its images, not its chrome.
Content
Discovery and planning tool, not promotional siteEvery page was structured so visitors can understand the concept, select an experience, and shape their visit before arriving. The site does not primarily exist to advertise — it exists to prepare. This distinction shaped every content and structural decision.
06 — Build

Coded from scratch.
Every decision visible.

The site was built in HTML5 and CSS, written in VS Code with version control via GitHub so every iteration is preserved and reviewable. Every structural decision from the IA phase translates directly into markup. Every typographic and spacing decision from the style guide becomes a CSS rule. Every copy decision determines what elements exist and how they're ordered.

The build phase is where accessibility decisions become concrete. Semantic HTML provides the structure screen readers depend on. Heading hierarchy, alt text, aria labels, and keyboard navigation were tested as part of the build — not added afterward as corrections.

Google Analytics was configured to track the three goal metrics from the research phase: click-through to How It Works, Experiences engagement and reservation conversions, and Community sign-ups. The build is not a standalone deliverable — it is the research made visible.

Accessibility — built in, not bolted on

WCAG Contrast

All text meets minimum contrast ratios against their backgrounds. Colour is not the only carrier of meaning — labels accompany the colour-coded accent bars throughout.

Semantic HTML

Proper heading hierarchy, landmark elements, and descriptive link text throughout. Structure communicates meaning to screen readers independently of visual layout.

Keyboard Navigation

All interactive elements operable without a mouse — navigation, filters, forms, dropdowns. Tab order follows visual layout. Focus states are visible.

Screen Reader Testing

What the research changed

Each phase left a specific, traceable mark on what was built — not general takeaways, but decisions that would have gone differently without them.

01
Structure is not neutral

How information is grouped changes how users think about it. The DOCR analysis made this concrete: without hierarchy, visitors interpret each piece in isolation. When structure aligns with how people already organise information, environments become easier to navigate and more meaningful to move through. Every IA decision in Botanic Elixir followed from this — navigation by intention, not by product category.

02
Heuristics reveal what conventions hide

Evaluating Heart Roasters, Wormhole, and SEY Coffee through NNG's ten heuristics surfaced what casual observation misses — carousels with no system status, typography that trades readability for aesthetics, external redirects that break continuity without warning. A structured framework turned gut reactions into decisions that could be defended.

03
Bias appears in the questions, not just the answers

Transcript review showed that bias enters through framing — "Is that how you currently keep track?" assumes the answer. "That sounds helpful" shapes what the participant says next. Audience assumptions for Botanic Elixir were treated as hypotheses to test, not truths to confirm. The research phase was about discovering actual behaviour.

04
Accessibility is structural, not remedial

Screen reader testing on the NYPL site showed how quickly missing alt text and unlabelled headings make navigation guesswork. SEY Coffee's small typography — a deliberate aesthetic choice — excludes users who need to zoom. For Botanic Elixir, accessibility was treated as a constraint from the outset. Not a checklist at the end.

05
Physical and digital spaces follow the same logic

Grouping, hierarchy, clarity, guidance — the principles that make digital interfaces navigable are the same ones that make physical spaces legible. Botanic Elixir came directly from this observation. Designing a café that responds to intention uses the same cognitive logic as designing a site that routes users toward what they need. The medium changes. The principles do not.

06
Language is the first design decision

Every word was written before a layout was finalised, because the label determines what structure is necessary. "How It Works" requires an explanatory page. "Experiences" requires a filterable grid. "Community" requires living content with a calendar. Copy was not added to the design — it was what the design was built from.
