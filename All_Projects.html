I had not used speech recognition technology before this assignment, so learning how to access it was a new experience. Reflection and Evaluation

Greatest Challenges

The most significant challenge was achieving exact formatting without manual corrections. The system showed inconsistencies in capitalization, punctuation placement, and number formatting. Words with homophones or lower frequency, such as "wok,"were particularly difficult. Error correction was also inefficient. Commands such as "backspace"removed characters one at a time, while "undo"often removed more text than intended. Additionally, instructing the system to use spelled-out numbers or numeric forms did not reliably work, as it sometimes transcribed the instruction itself instead of applying it.

Most Exciting Aspects

Once the command structure was understood, the technology could produce reasonably accurate text with minimal physical input. The ability to insert punctuation by voice and navigate text without a keyboard demonstrated strong potential for accessibility and hands-free interaction. When recognition succeeded, the speed and convenience were notable.

Overall Reflection

Speech recognition technology proved capable but inconsistent when precise formatting was required. It performed best with common vocabulary and straightforward sentence structures, but accuracy declined with uncommon words, formatting constraints, and strict output requirements. The experience highlighted both the practical usefulness of voice input and the limitations that still affect reliability and efficiency.

Screen Reader Structure Analysis

This study documents what happened when the New York Public Library site was explored through screen reader output alone. Instead of relying on visual hierarchy, the analysis reconstructs the page from headings, labels, and link announcements, making the site's structure visible through absence, repetition, and ambiguity.

The exercise revealed how much navigation depends on semantic clarity. When headings were sparse, labels were vague, or alt text was missing, the page no longer functioned as a coherent system. The result was not simply slower movement, but a different experience of the interface altogether - one based on inference rather than guidance.

Core focus
  • Heading logic and content grouping
  • Link clarity and navigation predictability
  • The effect of missing or vague alternative text
  • How audio-only navigation reshapes the mental model of the page
Page 2

Key Findings and Reflection

Findings

The site could still be traversed, but not with confidence. The structure depended too heavily on visual interpretation, which meant the screen reader user had to assemble the page manually from fragments. Navigation cues that are visually obvious did not consistently translate into meaningful spoken guidance.

Interpretation

This made the interface feel less like a designed path and more like a reconstruction task. Missing labels, weak headings, and limited descriptive support forced the user to infer relationships between content blocks that should have been directly communicated by the page itself.

Overall Reflection

The analysis shows that accessibility is not only about adding tools, but about preserving structure across modes of perception. When semantic hierarchy is weak, the experience fractures. A screen reader does not merely reveal missing accessibility support - it reveals whether the underlying design logic is actually coherent.

Origins, Evolution, and Contemporary Applications of Hypermedia and Hypertext

Introduction

The World Wide Web (WWW) is one of the primary digital environments through which information is accessed, organized, and interpreted. Hypertext and hypermedia are central to how this environment functions. Rather than structuring information as a fixed, linear sequence, hypertext organizes content through linked relationships. Hypermedia extends this concept by integrating multimedia elements within the same networked structure. Together, these systems determine how information is stored, retrieved, and understood across distributed networks.

Because of this central role, hypertext and hypermedia are not separate from the design of the WWW. Instead, they shape the architecture and experience of the web at a fundamental level. In this paper, I examine the historical development of hypertext and hypermedia, their integration into the WWW, and their continuing influence in contemporary digital contexts such as software engineering, narrative environments, and media distribution. Throughout this analysis, I emphasize how hypertext and hypermedia function not simply as tools, but as underlying design principles that inform how digital information systems are constructed and used.

Historical Origins of Hypermedia and Hypertext

Early Conceptualization and Development

The origins of hypertext precede the modern internet by several decades. One of the earliest complete implementations appeared in Douglas Engelbart's oN-Line System (NLS). This

Page 2

Historical Origins of Hypermedia and Hypertext

system did not present information linearly. Instead, users navigated through networks of connected content. As Bieber et al. (1997) describe, NLS allowed information to be accessed "non-sequentially through networks of interrelated nodes."This meant that meaning was generated through relationships between pieces of information rather than by reading in a predetermined order. Consequently, researchers began to consider how digital environments might support human cognition by reflecting how ideas are conceptually linked rather than arranged in a single path.

As hypertext matured conceptually, research expanded into hypermedia. This broader field sought not only to organize text, but to integrate multiple forms of information, such as images and audio, within the same relational framework (Bieber et al., 1997). This work provided the theoretical grounding for the web long before it existed as a public system.

The Creation of the World Wide Web

These theoretical developments became practically implemented in 1989, when Tim Berners-Lee and his colleagues at CERN developed the WWW. Their goal was to support distributed scientific collaboration. To achieve this, they created a hypertext-based system that allowed researchers to link, share, and retrieve documents across locations. As Candiasa (2022) notes, the WWW "offered a unified means of accessing diverse information sources through hypertext links,"making large-scale networked communication feasible.

Because of this design goal, hypertext was built into the web from the beginning. Khosrowpour and Herman (2001) describe the WWW as a "hypertext-based environment enabling global information distribution and retrieval."HTML and related web standards were therefore not neutral technical choices. They were created specifically to support collaborative

The Evolution to Hypermedia

As the WWW expanded, it evolved beyond text-only linking. Hypertext structures were extended into hypermedia through the integration of images, audio, video, and interactive elements. Candiasa (2022) defines hypermedia as "the combination of hypertext with multimedia elements such as images, video, and audio."This development shifted the web from a primarily textual information environment toward a multimodal communication system. Accordingly, Langegger et al. (2006) characterize the contemporary web as a "ubiquitous hypermedia system."This evolution illustrates how hypertext principles continue to scale as digital environments grow more complex.

Impact on Contemporary Web Architecture

Architectural Foundations and Design Principles

Hypertext does not only influence how users navigate information. It also shapes how the web is structured at the architectural level. Fielding and Taylor (2002) describe the WWW as an "internet-scale distributed hypermedia system,"emphasizing the need for scalability, consistency, and secure communication across independent components. To formalize these requirements, they developed the Representational State Transfer (REST) architectural model.

REST specifies how web systems should behave in order to remain efficient and evolvable. One important constraint within REST is hypermedia as the engine of application state (HATEOAS). This principle means that interactions within a system are driven by

Origins, Evolution, and Contemporary Applications of Hypermedia and Hypertext

Introduction

The World Wide Web (WWW) is one of the primary digital environments through which information is accessed, organized, and interpreted. Hypertext and hypermedia are central to how this environment functions. Rather than structuring information as a fixed, linear sequence, hypertext organizes content through linked relationships. Hypermedia extends this concept by integrating multimedia elements within the same networked structure. Together, these systems determine how information is stored, retrieved, and understood across distributed networks.

Because of this central role, hypertext and hypermedia are not separate from the design of the WWW. Instead, they shape the architecture and experience of the web at a fundamental level. In this paper, I examine the historical development of hypertext and hypermedia, their integration into the WWW, and their continuing influence in contemporary digital contexts such as software engineering, narrative environments, and media distribution. Throughout this analysis, I emphasize how hypertext and hypermedia function not simply as tools, but as underlying design principles that inform how digital information systems are constructed and used.

Historical Origins of Hypermedia and Hypertext

Early Conceptualization and Development

The origins of hypertext precede the modern internet by several decades. One of the earliest complete implementations appeared in Douglas Engelbart's oN-Line System (NLS). This

system did not present information linearly. Instead, users navigated through networks of connected content. As Bieber et al. (1997) describe, NLS allowed information to be accessed "non-sequentially through networks of interrelated nodes."This meant that meaning was generated through relationships between pieces of information rather than by reading in a predetermined order. Consequently, researchers began to consider how digital environments might support human cognition by reflecting how ideas are conceptually linked rather than arranged in a single path.

As hypertext matured conceptually, research expanded into hypermedia. This broader field sought not only to organize text, but to integrate multiple forms of information, such as images and audio, within the same relational framework (Bieber et al., 1997). This work provided the theoretical grounding for the web long before it existed as a public system.

The Creation of the World Wide Web

These theoretical developments became practically implemented in 1989, when Tim Berners-Lee and his colleagues at CERN developed the WWW. Their goal was to support distributed scientific collaboration. To achieve this, they created a hypertext-based system that allowed researchers to link, share, and retrieve documents across locations. As Candiasa (2022) notes, the WWW "offered a unified means of accessing diverse information sources through hypertext links,"making large-scale networked communication feasible.

Part 1: Identify the Problem. For each question below: 1. Identify what is flawed. 2. Rewrite to improve the question.

1. Flawed: "Do you find it frustrating when professors assign too much work?"What's wrong? The question is leading and assumes that professors assign excessive work and that the interviewee finds it frustrating. This may pressure the participant to agree or respond negatively, even if their experience differs. It also frames the issue emotionally rather than neutrally. Stronger Version: Can you describe your experience with the workload across your classes?

2. Flawed: "How often do you procrastinate?"What's wrong? The question assumes that the participant procrastinates and may make them feel judged or defensive. It also focuses on frequency without understanding underlying behaviors or context. Stronger Version: How do you typically manage your time when completing assignments?

Page 2

Question Critique "Continued

3. Flawed: "Would you use an app that helps organize your deadlines?"What's wrong? This question asks about hypothetical future behavior rather than actual experience. Participants may say they would use such an app even if they would not in reality. It also does not explore whether they currently use tools, what problems they face, or what features they need. Stronger Version: How do you currently keep track of your deadlines?

4. Flawed: "Is time management hard for you?"What's wrong? It is a closed and leading question. The person may feel defensive because the question assumes that time management is difficult for them. The question doesn't explore their strategies or their experiences; instead, it just asks a simple yes/no answer that doesn't provide us with any understanding or context. Stronger Version: Can you tell me about your experience managing your time and any approaches you've found helpful?

5. Flawed: "Why don't students plan ahead more?"What's wrong? This question assumes that students do not plan ahead and frames the issue in a judgmental way. It may cause participants to feel blamed or defensive and does not allow for the possibility that some students do plan ahead or face barriers that affect their ability to do so. Stronger Version: What factors have affected your studies, and how have you responded to them?

Hypothetical trap

Speculate less

Questions about what someone would do later often produce polished guesses instead of grounded evidence from real behavior.

Closed framing

Do not corner the answer

Yes or no questions collapse nuance and make it harder for the participant to describe strategies, barriers, and context.

Better move

Ask from lived practice

Neutral prompts about current behavior produce richer material because they invite stories, routines, and tradeoffs.

Repair principle: move away from hypothetical or judgmental framing and back toward present-tense lived experience.

Question Critique "Continued

6. Flawed: "You prefer digital calendars over paper planners, right?" What's wrong? This is a leading question that assumes the participant prefers digital calendars and encourages agreement through the phrase "right?" It may pressure the participant to confirm the interviewer's expectation rather than express their true preference. Do not push confirmation

Phrases like "right?" reward agreement and narrow the participant's freedom to describe a different preference or behavior.

Solution bias

Do not design too early

Feature questions jump straight to invention before the research has properly understood the participant's current conditions and needs.

Better follow-up

Probe the current system

Ask what tools the participant uses now, what they like about them, and where friction still appears before discussing solutions.

Interview Analysis

Conducting an interview with a peer provided valuable insight into both the participant's study habits and my own interviewing skills. While the conversation flowed comfortably, reviewing the transcript revealed areas where bias appeared, opportunities for deeper probing were missed, and improvements could be made for future interviews.

Bias appeared primarily in the form of leading or assumptive language. At several points, I framed questions in ways that suggested a preferred answer or reinforced what the participant had already said rather than inviting new information. For example, after the participant described using a digital calendar, I asked, "Is that how you currently keep track of your deadlines?"This question assumed that the digital calendar was the primary method, potentially limiting discussion of alternative strategies. Similarly, statements such as "That sounds very helpful"or "It seems like you do a good job"introduced positive evaluation, which may have influenced the participant to continue emphasizing successful strategies rather than discussing challenges or failures. These responses reflect confirmation bias, as I tended to validate information that aligned with the topic of time management tools instead of remaining neutral.

One missed probing opportunity occurred when the participant mentioned that deadlines used to "creep up"on them and cause lost sleep before they adopted the digital calendar. This was a moment where I could have explored the emotional, academic, or behavioral impact in more depth. Follow-up questions such as "How did that affect your academic performance?"or "What prompted you to change your system at that time?"could have provided richer data about motivation and consequences. Instead, I acknowledged the stress but moved on to the next prepared question, which limited the depth of insight gained.

Page 2

Interview Analysis "Reflection

Something that surprised me was how structured and sophisticated the participant's time-management system was. Rather than simply recording deadlines, they broke assignments into weekly tasks, used priority levels, notes, reminders, and multiple devices for synchronization. Additionally, the participant reported that reminders reduced anxiety rather than increasing it, which contrasts with the common assumption that frequent notifications can be stressful. This highlighted the importance of not generalizing user experiences with productivity tools.

In a future interview, I would improve several aspects of my approach. First, I would focus on asking more open-ended, neutral questions that do not imply a preferred response. Second, I would allow more space for elaboration by using probing techniques such as "Can you tell me more about that?"or "What was that experience like for you?"Third, I would minimize evaluative comments that could influence the participant's responses. Finally, I would practice active listening and flexibility rather than relying too heavily on a predetermined list of questions, allowing the conversation to follow meaningful themes as they emerge.

Overall, this interview demonstrated the importance of neutrality, curiosity, and responsiveness in qualitative data collection. While the participant provided useful information about time-management practices, a more exploratory approach would likely have produced deeper insights into their experiences and needs.

Page 2

Introduction

This report evaluates the accessibility, usability, and performance of the current website and proposes a redesigned approach that better aligns with established design standards. The analysis examines key factors that shape user experience, including visual hierarchy, color contrast, typography, structural organization, navigation, and mobile compatibility. Although the existing site presents a distinctive visual identity and strong atmospheric branding, several design choices reduce clarity, efficiency, and ease of use.

The proposed redesign addresses these issues through clearer sectioning, improved readability, stronger visual support, and a more coherent layout aligned with typical online browsing behavior. In addition, recommendations for responsive implementation and targeted code-level improvements are provided to enhance accessibility, performance, and long-term maintainability. The objective is not to change the brand identity but to ensure that the site communicates information effectively while enabling rapid comprehension, intuitive navigation, and a consistent experience across devices.

Page 3

Why the Current Site Does Not Meet Standards

The current site does not meet established accessibility, usability, and performance standards because it prioritizes stylistic impact without maintaining coherence, structural order, or consistent design principles. Rather than functioning as a unified interface, the page appears as a sequence of visually driven sections lacking a stable system for size, color, typography, or layout. Consequently, users must interpret the site piece by piece instead of being guided through a cohesive experience; this increases cognitive effort and reduces efficiency (Norman, 2013).

These issues are particularly evident in the site's use of color and contrast. Background colors shift abruptly between sections without a consistent palette or visual rhythm, which weakens hierarchy and creates a sense of unpredictability. At the same time, text contrast is often insufficient; for example, light gray text on white backgrounds or dark text on dark backgrounds. Such combinations reduce readability and may fail WCAG contrast guidelines, creating barriers for users with low vision, color vision deficiencies, or those viewing the site in bright environments (W3C, 2018).

Structural organization further contributes to usability problems. Content appears in an order that does not align with typical user goals, such as exploring offerings, understanding the brand, or taking action. Promotional content, educational material, membership prompts, location details, and service information are interspersed without clear transitions or an intentional narrative flow. Because section headings are missing, visually understated, or unclear, users must infer the purpose of each segment and its relevance within the page. When information is not meaningfully grouped, users expend additional mental effort to construct their own understanding of the interface (Sweller, 1988).

Page 4

Navigation, Typography, and Layout Failure

As a result, navigation and mobility are also compromised. Movement through the landing page relies heavily on continuous scrolling rather than clearly defined pathways. Without anchor links, consistent headers, or orientation cues, users cannot easily jump to relevant information or return to previously viewed sections. This limitation is especially problematic on mobile devices, where long scrolling experiences require strong visual signposting to prevent disorientation. When internal navigation support is weak, task completion becomes slower and more frustrating, particularly on smaller screens where navigation options are constrained (Nielsen, 1994).

Typography further intensifies these challenges. Body text frequently appears below recommended readable sizes, especially in sections dominated by large imagery and generous spacing. Combined with low contrast and inconsistent spacing, this results in text that is visually fatiguing and difficult to scan. Moreover, headings, subheadings, and body copy are not clearly differentiated, which weakens hierarchy and makes it difficult to identify key information. A well-defined typographic structure is widely recognized as essential for supporting scanning behavior and comprehension in content-rich interfaces (Lupton, 2010).

The overall layout reflects the absence of a coherent design system. Variations in spacing, alignment, and section density create an uneven visual rhythm across the page. Some areas contain excessive empty space, while others compress information into small blocks of text; this produces an unbalanced experience. Although imagery is used extensively, it does not consistently support user decision making; in several cases, visuals appear decorative rather than informative, adding visual weight without improving understanding.

Page 5

What Is Being Changed and Why?

The redesigned page introduces a clearer structure, improved visual hierarchy, and meaningful visual support to enhance comprehension and usability. While the original page contained three brewing categories, the coffees listed under each section were presented primarily as undifferentiated text, making it difficult to distinguish individual offerings or compare options. The redesign separates these listings into distinct visual groupings and presents a curated selection of coffees recommended for each brewing style. Some coffees appear in multiple sections, reflecting their versatility across preparation methods. This approach mirrors real-world coffee guidance, where certain coffees perform well across multiple brewing techniques and where users benefit from evaluating several suitable options within a single view (Norman, 2013).

A prominent header was added to establish context immediately upon entry. The title and background imagery communicate the purpose of the page, displaying what is currently being brewed, without requiring interpretation. Providing this immediate orientation supports widely recognized usability principles that emphasize clarity of purpose and recognition over recall, allowing users to understand how to proceed without additional effort (Nielsen, 1994).

Within each section, images of the coffee packaging were added as functional elements rather than decorative ones. These visuals help users quickly differentiate between coffees, recognize familiar products, and understand which options are recommended for a given brewing method. Importantly, the design does not imply exclusivity between coffees and methods; instead, it communicates guidance. A coffee may appear in more than one section if it is appropriate for multiple preparation styles.

Page 6

Responsive or Adaptive Design?

Responsive web design uses a single layout that adjusts fluidly to the viewing environment through flexible grids, scalable media, and CSS breakpoints. The underlying content structure remains consistent while the presentation reorganizes continuously as the screen size changes. This approach is widely recommended when consistency, accessibility, and cross-device continuity are priorities.

Adaptive web design, by contrast, provides multiple predefined layouts designed for specific device categories. The system detects characteristics such as screen size or device type and loads the most appropriate version. Adaptive approaches are often advantageous when devices differ substantially in capability or when performance optimization is critical.

Applying these principles to the redesigned DOCR coffee page, responsive design provides the strongest foundation. The interface consists primarily of structured content elements such as product cards, images, and concise descriptions, all of which can reorganize effectively across screen sizes without losing meaning.

At the same time, certain aspects of the interface benefit from adaptive refinement. Navigation systems must accommodate touch input and limited screen space on mobile devices, where precise pointer control is unavailable. This combined approach allows the DOCR site to communicate offerings clearly and efficiently regardless of device while maintaining a cohesive visual identity and user experience.

Page 7

Examples of How Code Would Be Changed or Added

Although the brewing-method page is relatively short, targeted code improvements can significantly enhance clarity, accessibility, and speed of comprehension. The primary purpose of this page is not extensive interaction but quick understanding of which coffees are recommended for each preparation style. Accordingly, code modifications focus on strengthening structure, readability, and differentiation while minimizing cognitive effort.

First, the page would use semantic HTML so each brewing method is treated as a clearly defined section of content. Espresso, Batch Brew, and Pour Over would each have a section heading coded as a true heading, such as <h2>, rather than as styled text alone. The coffees listed under each method would then be grouped as individual entries, for example as repeatable <article> blocks. This improves accessibility because screen readers and other assistive tools rely on headings and section structure to help users move through a page efficiently.

Second, consistent card-based containers would be implemented for each coffee listing. A uniform layout that includes image, name, origin or producer, and tasting notes allows users to compare options directly without needing to interpret different formatting styles.

To further support orientation, the title of the active brewing section would be visually emphasized. When a section is selected or brought into view, its heading would be enclosed within a clearly defined container and highlighted through contrast, background shading, or typographic emphasis. Readability would also be improved through accessible typographic settings defined in CSS.

Page 8

Conclusion

Overall, the analysis indicates that while the current site presents a distinctive visual identity, it does not consistently support accessibility, readability, or efficient task completion. Inconsistent contrast, weak structural organization, and unclear navigation make it difficult for users to quickly understand available information or move through the page with confidence.

The proposed redesign addresses these issues by establishing a clearer hierarchy, stronger sectioning, improved contrast, and purposeful visual support. Content is organized around typical user goals and presented in consistent patterns that facilitate scanning and comparison. Responsive design serves as the primary implementation strategy to maintain continuity across devices, while targeted adaptive adjustments support usability in contexts where interaction constraints differ.

Code-level improvements further reinforce these changes by enabling semantic structure, scalable layouts, and efficient content delivery. Together, these enhancements transform the page into a coherent, approachable interface that communicates recommendations quickly and clearly.
